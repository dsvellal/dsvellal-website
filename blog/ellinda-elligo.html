Ellinda Elligo!! (From Where to Where!!)

May 24, 2008

I always wanted to write a story. Recently, going through my old diaries, I accidentally bumped into a story written by me — about 10-12 pages, hand written, from just after finishing my 10th!

Multiple thoughts came flooding in — and I decided to jot them down:

  1. How enthusiastic I was back then
  2. How small things used to fascinate me — why they don't now!
  3. Will I ever complete the story?
  4. How much I have changed
  5. My ability to get lost in thoughts
  6. Can I type at the speed of my thoughts?

Look at these thoughts for a moment. The thought-chain started with "how enthusiastic I was", and within two minutes ended up with "can I type at the speed of my thoughts." They say the only thing that can go faster than light is the mind — its infinite capabilities have not been explored yet.

Many people have explored controlling thoughts — through "meditation" or "yoga." The methods all focus on one thing: Observation.

Have you noticed how behaviour changes if you observe? Try observing yourselves. There are three stages to change:

  1. The behaviour
  2. Recognize the behaviour — the most important step
  3. Gradual change

Many times I've noticed that I get so involved in the behaviour, I become one with it, instead of recognizing it. If I am angry, I am so much involved in being angry that I really don't think that I am angry. But if I can "recognize" that I am angry, the effect drastically reduces.

When angry, I think of — why am I angry? Is there a problem? Does it require me to be angry? Will what I say affect someone negatively? Are there other solutions?

It's like pausing a movie of my life: the "first me" is still angry but paused, the "second me" is thinking through the questions, and the "third me" observes both — and when the third me smiles, I unpause my movie, and surprisingly I have a smile on my face.

They say if you observe, even a single-celled organism behaves differently. This is precisely why our elders chose to observe their thoughts — and that is precisely what "meditation" aims at — observing the inner self :)
