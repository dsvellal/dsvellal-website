God, Religion and Wikipedia!

January 17, 2010

During one of the yoga classes, a very interesting question was raised: "How does Wikipedia work?" The answer ties beautifully into spiritual philosophy.

Wikipedia runs on volunteer contributions. Anyone can write, edit, and refine content. There's no monetary reward. People contribute knowledge purely for the satisfaction of sharing it. And yet, it's one of the most accurate and comprehensive knowledge bases in the world.

Now consider the Bhagavad Gita's concept of Karma Yoga:

"KarmaNye vaadhikaarasthe maa phaleshu kadaachana" — You have the right to work, but never to the fruit of that work.

Wikipedia contributors embody this principle! They do their work (writing and editing) without expecting reward (no payment). The result is extraordinary — a world-class encyclopedia built entirely on selfless action.

In religious contexts, God is often described as one who:

  • Gives without expecting returns
  • Maintains order and balance
  • Serves as a guiding principle

Wikipedia's moderation system mirrors this. The community self-corrects. Bad edits get reverted. Good contributions survive. Truth emerges through collective effort — not through authority, but through voluntary adherence to a shared purpose.

Just as religion provides a framework for ethical living, Wikipedia provides a framework for knowledge sharing. Both work because enough people genuinely care about the outcome without caring about personal gain.

The next time someone questions whether selfless action works — point them to Wikipedia. It's Karma Yoga in action, running 24/7, serving billions, built by volunteers who give without expecting anything in return.
