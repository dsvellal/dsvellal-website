Justice Tempered with Mercy

February 20, 2012

Shakespeare said in The Merchant of Venice:

"The quality of mercy is not strained. It droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven upon the place beneath. It is twice blessed — it blesseth him that gives and him that takes."

Here is a story that illustrates this beautifully.

A king had a magnificent horse — the pride of his kingdom. One night, a thief stole the horse. The king's soldiers caught him the very next day.

The thief was brought before the king. The law was clear — theft of royal property meant death. The ministers demanded the harshest punishment.

But the king studied the thief. He was young, perhaps 20. His clothes were torn, his body thin. Desperation, not malice, drove him.

The king asked, "Why did you steal my horse?"

The boy trembled. "My lord, my mother is very sick. I needed money for her medicine. I thought I could sell the horse and save her. I know it was wrong. I accept any punishment."

The ministers whispered, "Execute him. The law is the law."

The king paused. Then he spoke:

"This boy stole for his mother. His crime is born of love, not greed. I shall not execute love. Here is my judgment: the horse is returned. This boy shall be appointed as a stable hand in my palace. He shall earn an honest living, and his mother shall receive treatment from the royal physician."

The court was stunned. The boy fell at the king's feet, tears streaming.

Years later, that same boy became the most loyal soldier in the kingdom's army — the one who saved the king's life in battle.

Justice tempered with mercy — when you judge with compassion, you don't just serve justice. You transform lives. The king didn't just save a boy. He created a protector. That is the mark of true wisdom.
