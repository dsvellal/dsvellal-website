An Afternoon Stroll

December 22, 2011

One afternoon I was taking a stroll. I noticed an old man, perhaps 80 years old, digging the earth near his house. Curiosity led me to him.

"Ajja (Grandfather), what are you doing?"

He smiled. "I am planting a coconut tree, my child."

I was puzzled. "But a coconut tree takes about 8-10 years to bear fruit. At your age..." I stopped, realizing how rude I sounded.

He didn't flinch. Instead, his smile widened.

"Son, every coconut I have eaten in my life was from a tree planted by someone before me. My grandfather planted trees whose fruits I enjoyed. My parents did the same. Now it is my turn. This tree is not for me — it is for you, your children, and their children."

He continued: "The problem with your generation is that you eat the fruit but forget to plant the tree. Everything is transactional. If I don't plant it, who will?"

I was speechless. This 80-year-old man was planting something he would probably never benefit from. And he was doing it with such joy, such contentment, such a natural sense of duty.

Giving back without expecting returns.

That conversation stayed with me. It became a principle I try to live by — in my professional life through mentoring, in my personal life through volunteering, and in every interaction where I can plant a seed today for someone else's tomorrow.

Plant your tree. You may not sit under its shade, but someone will. And that someone will smile, just like you smiled under the tree your grandfather planted.
