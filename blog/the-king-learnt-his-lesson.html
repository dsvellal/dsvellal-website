...And Then the King Learnt His Lesson!

December 28, 2008

Long ago, there lived a king. He was rich beyond anyone's dream — a prosperous kingdom, loyal subjects, kind wife, noble ministers, witty son. But he was not happy. For some strange reason, he was always worried about what-will-go-wrong-tomorrow.

A wise man visited his kingdom. The king explained his problem. The wise man said, "I have a solution, but you must perform two tasks for me."

First task: "Walk around your palace holding this spoon full of oil, without spilling a drop." The king walked very carefully and returned. "What did you see?" asked the wise man. "Nothing, sir. I was so involved in not spilling the oil, I couldn't concentrate on other things."

Second task: "Walk again with the spoon, but this time, look around and tell me what you see." The king returned. "I saw how big my castle was, the carvings on the wall, how organized my soldiers were. I also observed my garden, the flowers, their smell, the cool breeze, the birds, their sweet chirpings, the sky — it was all very pleasing."

The wise man replied:

"...and yet, you still hold the spoon filled with oil. In your first task, you concentrated so hard on the oil that you failed to see other things. In your second, you opened up and started looking at places that made you realize there is a world beyond the spoon in your hand."
"This is the same thing happening in your life. You concentrate on things that are yet-to-happen, and forget to see the present. Open up your senses and start living in the present."

After my own para-gliding experience where I spent half the time worrying about the hooks, I realized: people know their limits. To enjoy things to the fullest, I have to learn to trust. Close your eyes, feel the air brushing through your face, convince your mind — the driver knows the vehicle's limits — so relax, and just enjoy the experience.

Try enjoying cooking without worrying about how others will react by tasting it. Or just say thank you to your bus driver without worrying about what your colleagues think. It really works!
