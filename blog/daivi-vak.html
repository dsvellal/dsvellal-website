Daivi Vak — The Celestial Voices

July 18, 2011

One of the most profound stories from the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad speaks about the three celestial instructions given to three different classes of beings — Gods, Men, and Demons.

Prajapathi, the creator, called each group and uttered a single syllable: "DA."

To the Gods (devas), "DA" meant Damyata — control yourselves. The gods, who had everything, needed discipline and restraint.

To Men (manushyas), "DA" meant Datta — give, be charitable. Humans, driven by desire, needed the virtue of giving.

To the Demons (asuras), "DA" meant Dayadvam — be compassionate. Demons, with their capacity for cruelty, needed empathy and mercy.

The same sound. Three different meanings. Each perfectly tailored to what the listener needed most.

This story fascinated T.S. Eliot so deeply that he used it in his masterpiece "The Waste Land" (1922), in the final section called "What the Thunder Said":em>

DA
Datta: what have we given?

DA
Dayadhvam: I have heard the key
Turn in the door once and turn once only

DA
Damyata: The boat responded
Gaily, to the hand expert with sail and oar

Shantih shantih shantih

Eliot understood what Vedic seers knew millennia ago: the deepest truths are universal. Give. Be compassionate. Control yourself. These three instructions cover the entire spectrum of human spiritual development.

And the thunder still says "DA" — the question is, what do you hear?
